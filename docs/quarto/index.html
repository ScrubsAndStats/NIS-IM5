Racial and Socioeconomic Impact on Mortality in Pregnant Hypertensive Women: Insights from the National Inpatient Sample

Analysis for RCOP NIS IM5

Author
Affiliation

Ali Salman

Dow University of Health Sciences

Preamble:

  • Reference Papers:

    • Thomas et al. (2017)
    • Mazza et al. (2022)
    • Zahid et al. (2022)

  • Study Objective: Investigate the influence of race and socioeconomic factors on pregnant inpatient admissions with a history of hypertension undergoing hospital delivery.

  • Data Source: Cross-sectional analysis of the National Inpatient Sample (NIS) from 2018 to 2020.

  • Patient Selection: Included all inpatient admissions with an ICD-10 diagnosis or procedure code for pregnancy delivery and a diagnosis of hypertension, whether chronic (pre-existing) or pregnancy-associated.

  • Primary Outcomes:

    • In-hospital all-cause mortality

    • Total hospital length of stay (days)

    • Total charge, inflation adjusted to 2020 ($) [adjusted to 2020 dollars, using CPI data by the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics]

  • Statistical Analysis: Multiple logistic and linear regression to determine the independent association of race and socioeconomic factors on outcomes, adjusted for:

    • Demographics: Age, race, insurance, residential income, residential region, hospital bedsize, hospital location/teaching status, and hospital census region.

    • Medical History: Hypertension type.

    • Comorbidities: Charlson comorbidity index, body mass index, diabetes mellitus, polycystic ovary syndrome, smoking status, alcohol use, and illicit drug use.

  • Software: All analyses were performed using R Version 4.4.1 (R Foundation for Statistical Computing, Vienna, Austria)
